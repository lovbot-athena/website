Ball-Approach Control of the Offense Robot

-->

Our offensive robot's main role is to get behind the ball, line up with the opponent's goal, and push. One part we focused on in our work is getting behind the ball. If the robot drives straight at the ball, it shoves it in whatever direction the robot happens to be facing, which is almost never toward the goal. What we actually want is a curved approach that loops the robot around the ball so that, by the time it makes contact, it is already pointed at the target. Here’s the story of how we developed the code:

Background

Every control loop, the IR eye gives us two numbers: the ball's bearing $\theta$ (in compass convention, $0^\circ$ straight ahead, increasing clockwise through $[0^\circ, 360^\circ)$) and a proximity reading $V$ from the IR signal strength, where a larger value means the ball is closer. From these we compute a singl output, the heading we actually drive:

$$\theta_{\text{final}} = \theta + (\text{sideways offset}).$$

The offset is what bends the path. Driving at $\theta$ with zero offset makes the robot charge the ball head-on; adding an offset aims it slightly to one side of it, which sweeps the robot around. The side we lean toward depends on the relative location of the ball:

$$s = \operatorname{sgn}(180^\circ - \theta) = \begin{cases} +1 & \theta \lt 180^\circ \ \text{(ball on the right)}\\ -1 & \theta \gt 180^\circ \ \text{(ball on the left)}\end{cases}$$

The engineering problem we’re solving is choosing how large that offset should be at any given moment. Three versions of answers follow.

Version 1: a fixed ratio

The first version used a constant. We picked a single fraction $\rho_0 \in [0,1]$ of the maximum $90^\circ$ offset and applied it everywhere:

$$\theta_{\text{final}} = \theta + s \cdot 90^\circ \cdot \rho_0.$$

This is the simplest thing that produces a curve, and we handled it with a separate if statement that set the offset to a fixed value depending on where the ball was. It taught us immediately why a constant cannot work. The amount of curving we need is not constant. When the ball is far off to the side, we want a large lateral component so the robot swings wide and comes around. When the robot is nearly on top of the ball and already lined up, we want the offset to collapse toward zero so it drives straight through. A single fixed offset forces one compromise for both situations, and because it switched in an if statement rather than scaling smoothly, the change was abrupt. As a result, the robot kept overshooting the ball, swinging too hard when it should have. We did not like this behavior and this pushed us to find something that is more dynamic.

Version 2: a square-root law

The fix was to make the offset depend on the measurements instead of being constant. We introduced a normalized proximity term,

$$u = \operatorname{clamp}\!\left(\frac{V - V_{\min}}{V_{\max} - V_{\min}},\ 0,\ 1\right),$$

with $V_{\min} \approx 10$ and $V_{\max} \approx 70$ taken from the eye's working range, and then scaled it through a power law rather than feeding it in linearly:

$$\rho(u) = u^{\,p}, \qquad p \approx 0.65 \ \ (\text{a pure square root is } p = 0.5).$$

We combined this with a normalized off-center angle $\alpha_{\text{lin}} = \operatorname{clamp}(\beta / 135^\circ,\ 0,\ 1)$, where $\beta$ is the ball's angular distance from straight ahead, giving

$$\theta_{\text{final}} = \theta + s \cdot 90^\circ \cdot u^{\,p} \cdot \alpha_{\text{lin}}.$$

The reason a square-root-style curve beats a linear one is its shape. A concave function like $u^{0.65}$ has a steep slope near zero and a shallow slope near one, so it front-loads the response: even a modest amount of proximity or off-centeredness produces a large early correction, and the response then eases off as the robot converges. In practice this gave us an aggressive swing when the robot was far from or beside the ball, tapering into a gentle, controlled line-up as it closed in. It was a substantial improvement, and for a while it was good enough.

However, this solution also has the weaknesses of any open-loop map. The function $\rho(u)$ is a fixed lookup from sensor reading to offset, with no memory and no sense of how the robot is actually behaving. It carries no integral term, so a small persistent misalignment never gets corrected; the robot can settle into a slightly-wrong approach and stay there. It carries no rate term, so when the robot's own momentum lags the command, the path can still overshoot and wobble. And the exponent $p$ is hand-tuned, so a value that feels right on one surface or at one speed drifts out of tune on another. Most of all, it ignores disturbances. A bump, or the ball drifting as we approach, throws off the static map with nothing to pull us back.

Version 3: PID

The final version replaced the static distance map with a closed-loop PID controller. Instead of reading the proximity and looking up an offset, we now define an error against a target engagement reading $V_{\text{set}}$ (the distance at which we want to be tracking the ball) and let a controller drive that error to zero:

$$e(t) = V_{\text{set}} - V(t),$$

$$D(t) = \operatorname{clamp}\!\Big(K_p\,e(t) + K_i\!\int_0^t \! e(\tau)\,d\tau + K_d\,\frac{de(t)}{dt},\ \ 0,\ 100\Big),$$

with our tuned gains $K_p = 2.5$, $K_i = 0$, $K_d = 0.010$ and the controller running in reverse-acting mode. The output $D \in [0,100]$ replaces the old $u^{\,p}$ as the distance scaling. We also upgraded the angle term from a clamped line to a saturating exponential, which removes the hard corner the linear clamp produced at its boundary:

$$\alpha = \frac{180^\circ - \lvert\,\theta - 180^\circ\,\rvert}{180^\circ} \in [0,1], \qquad g(\alpha) = 1 - e^{-k\alpha}, \quad k = 3.$$

Putting the two together gives the steering law the robot runs today:

$$\theta_{\text{final}} = \theta + \operatorname{sgn}(180^\circ - \theta)\cdot 90^\circ \cdot \frac{D}{100}\cdot\left(1 - e^{-k\alpha}\right).$$

Or written out in full, with the distance PID expanded and the $90/100$ scaling folded into $9/10$, the entire law reads as a single self-contained expression:

$$\theta_{\text{final}}(t) = \theta + \operatorname{sgn}(180^\circ-\theta)\cdot\frac{9}{10}\left[K_p\,e(t) + K_i\!\int_0^t \! e(\tau)\,d\tau + K_d\,\frac{de(t)}{dt}\right]\left(1 - \exp\!\left(-\,k\cdot\frac{180^\circ - \lvert\theta - 180^\circ\rvert}{180^\circ}\right)\right)$$

Each piece of the controller earns its place. The proportional term $K_p e$ produces an immediate correction in proportion to how far we are from the desired engagement distance, which is the responsiveness that the fixed ratio lacked. The derivative term $K_d\,\dot e$ reacts to the rate the error is changing and acts as a brake, easing the correction off before the robot overshoots and damping the oscillation that the open-loop map could not suppress; our small $K_d = 0.010$ supplies exactly that light damping without making the response sluggish. The integral term $K_i$ accumulates persistent error and would erase any steady-state offset that the proportional term alone leaves behind; we currently run it at zero and lean on P and D, which leaves a clear lever for future tuning. Because the whole thing is a feedback loop, it measures what the robot is actually doing every cycle and corrects against disturbances that the static square-root law simply absorbed.

The exponential angle gain $g(\alpha)$ complements this. It behaves almost linearly for small $\alpha$, so tiny misalignments produce proportionally tiny offsets, then saturates smoothly toward one as the ball moves far off-center, which guarantees the offset can never exceed the intended $90^\circ$ regardless of input. The constant $k = 3$ sets how quickly that saturation arrives.

One last detail keeps the loop honest. The moment the robot reaches the ball, we call a reset that re-initializes the controller's output limits and clears its accumulated state. This prevents integral windup from carrying stale correction into the next pursuit, so every fresh approach begins from a clean slate.
